How to Play

Quick version: take turns, answer questions, and race through the brain to the finish.

👥

Take Turns

This is a 2-player game. Players alternate turns the whole time, so only one person acts at once.

🎲

Roll and Move

On your turn, roll the die and move 1–3 spaces forward along the brain path.

❓

Answer a Question

After you land on a space, you answer one learning and conditioning question.

⏱️

If You’re Correct

You unlock a choice: take a small reward right away, or wait for a bigger delayed reward.

🚫

If You’re Wrong

You lose the move from that turn and go back to the space where you started your turn.

⚡

Cue Spaces

Some spaces have a ⚡ cue. Early on, it predicts a bonus. Later it becomes less reliable, and eventually it stops helping.

The Three Conditions

Condition 1
Acquisition — the delayed reward is worth it, and the cue works consistently.
Condition 2
Increased Delay — the delayed reward still exists, but now it takes longer, and the cue is less reliable.
Condition 3
Extinction — the delayed reward stops paying off, and the cue no longer gives a bonus.

What we think will happen

  • Players will choose the delayed reward more when the wait is short.
  • Players will shift toward the immediate reward when the delay gets longer.
  • Players may keep trying the delayed option for a bit, even after it stops working.
  • The cue may still affect behavior after it stops predicting a reward.
  • Players who miss questions will lose progress because they do not keep the move from that turn.

Interpretation

How to Play

Two players take turns. On your turn, roll the die and move forward.

Then answer a question. If you get it right, you unlock a choice between a small reward now or a bigger reward later.

If you get it wrong, you lose that move and go back to the space where you started your turn.

Watch the ⚡ cue spaces. Early in the game they help a lot, but later they stop being reliable.

The board has three conditions: acquisition, increased delay, and extinction.

At the end, the game compares how both players behaved across the conditions.
